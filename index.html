Plataforma

A cooperative network for discovering and cataloging creative work — built with and for artists.

The problem

Artists are hard to find. A personal portfolio site is discoverable only if someone already knows the artist's name. Gallery websites showcase their own roster but not the wider landscape. Existing platforms are marketplaces first — discovery is a byproduct of selling, not a goal in itself. There is no place where a curator, collector, researcher, or fellow artist can explore creative work across a community the way one might browse a great library or walk through a well-curated fair.

Meanwhile, the infrastructure that does exist is under pressure. The pandemic made clear how precarious support for artists is, and the situation in 2026 is no better: AI threatens ownership, public grants are being divested, and social media demands that artists "game" algorithms in the hope of finding financial benefactors — a losing proposition for most.

Artists, though famously under-organized, are increasingly aware of the need to create solutions for themselves.

The vision

Plataforma is a cooperative directory where artists are discoverable — by curators, collectors, other artists, and the public — within a space that has the editorial seriousness of a good institution and the reach of the open web. Not a marketplace. Not a social network. A structured, searchable, browsable map of creative production across disciplines, built and governed by the artists themselves.

Each artist maintains a catalog of their work. But cataloging is just the mechanism — the point is what happens when catalogs are connected. A visitor can arrive looking for one artist and discover ten more through shared exhibitions, institutions, movements, or media. The platform makes the relationships between artists, works, and institutions visible and navigable in a way that no individual portfolio site or gallery roster can.

How it works

The platform does the cataloging work for you. When you join, Plataforma searches the collections of major museums, Wikidata, and other public databases for works already attributed to you, and presents them for your review. You confirm, correct, or add to what it finds. The experience is "we found 47 of your works in museum collections — confirm?" rather than "please enter your first artwork."

This is possible because museums have been digitizing their collections for decades. The Metropolitan Museum has 480,000 records with open metadata. The Smithsonian has 11 million. The Rijksmuseum, 700,000. Plataforma draws from twelve major open-access collections and growing.

Your images come from you. Museum open-access programs overwhelmingly cover metadata — titles, dates, exhibition histories, collection placements — but restrict images of contemporary work behind copyright. The record that your sculpture was acquired by MoMA in 2019 is public data; the photograph of the sculpture usually is not. This means your career history can be auto-populated from public sources, but the visual representation of your work is something only you can provide. That's by design: your images, your control.

Identity across systems. Scientists have ORCID — a universal identifier that follows them across institutions. Artists have nothing comparable. Plataforma links your profile to every existing identifier (Wikidata, Getty ULAN, ISNI, MusicBrainz) and gives you a persistent, portable URI. Long term, we aim to become an identity standard for creative professionals — an ORCID for artists.

Not just visual art. The catalog handles creative work in any discipline. A painting has dimensions; a composition has duration and performers; a film has runtime, cast, and screenings. The platform draws from museum collections, MusicBrainz, film databases, and Wikidata. An artist working across media — or a musician, filmmaker, or architect — can maintain a single catalog of their entire body of work.

What's different

You own your data. The platform is a custodian, not an owner. You grant a revocable license to display and index your work. If you leave, your data is deleted — not archived, not retained. You can export everything, anytime, in open formats.

No training without consent. If the platform ever builds recommendation tools or intelligence from artist data, you opt in explicitly. Consent is granular and revocable.

Artists govern the platform. Plataforma is a cooperative. The people whose work constitutes the platform make the rules. Policies are set by members, not investors.

If value is created, it flows to you. If the platform generates revenue from your data — through recommendations, licensing, or future sales features — compensation is tracked and distributed to contributors, not captured by the platform.

These are not terms-of-service promises. They are structural commitments, designed into the architecture and enforced by cooperative governance. For the reasoning behind each commitment, see the founders' concept.

The approach

Plataforma grows through Pods — cohorts of artists who join as a group with one or two leaders who are early adopters. Pods are built around existing connections between artists and their interest in sharing those collaborations publicly, alongside the archive of their work.

Starting with New York City artists and institutions for the initial build phase, then expanding along the international networks of participating NYC artists. The first pods will be drawn from the founders' personal networks, starting with Okwui Okpokwasili and the Sweat Variant team.
