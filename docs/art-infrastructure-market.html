The art infrastructure market

A six-layer stack, with money flowing upward from the buyer and rights flowing downward from the artist. Each layer has incumbent players, a technology pattern, and an extractive logic that shapes who can build what next.

Layer 1 — Production

The artist makes the work. Studio, materials, time. Roughly 300,000 working visual artists in the US alone, ~10× that globally if you include hobbyists with ambition.

The economic reality: the long-tail distribution is brutal. The top 0.1% of living artists earn ~70% of the market; the median professional artist makes < $30k from art. The rest are subsidizing their practice with teaching, side jobs, or partner income. Layers 2–6 exist on top of Layer 1's economic precarity — the value chain extracts upward, and artists don't capture much of the value their work generates downstream.

Layer 2 — Representation (primary market)

Galleries take artists on, mount shows, sell. Standard commission: 50/50 split. Galleries provide visibility, social capital, market-making, archival photography, art-fair access, collector relationships.

Tiers:

  • Mega-galleries — Gagosian, Pace, Hauser & Wirth, Zwirner, White Cube. ~$1B+ collective annual revenue. Closed ecosystems with hundreds of staff. Inventory at Gagosian alone est. 100k+ works under management.
  • Major galleries — Marian Goodman, Lehmann Maupin, Petzel, Victoria Miro, etc. ~50–200 employees, multiple locations.
  • Mid-tier — Bortolami, Andrew Kreps, James Cohan. Single gallery, ~5–20 staff.
  • Emerging / project spaces — Karma, dozens of NYC project spaces.

The mega-gallery consolidation is recent — Larry Gagosian (b. 1945) is still alive and the market is positioning for the post-Gagosian era. Several mid-tier galleries close every year; consolidation is inexorable.

Software substrate. Artlogic + Exhibit-E (post-merger) is the dominant gallery CMS at the major-and-mid-tier — hundreds of galleries on this stack. ArtBinder (mobile-first inventory) is the secondary. Many galleries also use Squarespace for their public site.

Layer 3 — Auction + secondary market

Auction houses sell works whose owner wants out. Commission structure: buyer's premium (often 26–28% on top of hammer price) + seller's commission. Estimated ~$15B/yr global auction market in fine art alone.

Three giants: Sotheby's, Christie's, Phillips. Below them, regional auction houses and online-first auctioneers (Heritage, Bonhams, Artnet Auctions, LiveAuctioneers).

Auction archives are a massive proprietary data asset — Christie's has 250+ years of sale records, Sotheby's similar. They don't license bulk access externally because pricing data is the moat. Artnet's Price Database (~14M auction records, subscription) is the closest thing to public access, but it's API-key gated and expensive. Artprice has similar.

Online secondary marketplaces: 1stDibs, Saatchi Art, Artspace, Artsy's secondary tier. Lower-end online: eBay's art category. Web3 attempts (OpenSea, SuperRare for digital-native works) largely collapsed as a primary market 2022–2024.

Layer 4 — Discovery + aggregation

The market layer that puts "what exists" in one place. This is where Beowolff lives.

The big two: Artsy (founded 2009, raised ~$130M, recently merged with Artnet under Beowolff Capital) and Artnet (founded 1989, public on Frankfurt exchange until acquired). Together they form the dominant commercial-discovery layer — Artsy = primary market focus, Artnet = secondary + price data.

Adjacent: MutualArt (subscription artist tracking), Artland (gallery-friendly listings), Wikipedia / Wikidata (open structured data, partial), Google Arts & Culture (closed ecosystem, scale, but no commercial layer), Pinterest and Instagram (de-facto discovery via algorithmic feed, not artist-controlled).

The entire layer is structurally extractive: galleries pay listing fees; aggregators take transaction commission; collectors get free-ish access subsidized by the listing-fee side. Artsy charges galleries ~$300–600/mo for basic listing + ~10–25% commission on online sales. Artnet's similar.

The Wolff/Yin pitch positions Beowolff as a recommendation layer that improves Artsy/Artnet's existing function rather than replacing it — visual similarity at embedding scale (vs. tag-based search) makes the existing inventory more findable. Plataforma sits adjacent to this layer as a cooperative alternative: same discovery role, different governance.

Layer 5 — Authentication + provenance

Who confirms a work is real and tracks its history.

Old-world: catalogues raisonnés (per-artist scholarly volumes, frozen in print or in slow-updated archives), auction-house specialists who authenticate for fee, artist foundations that hold the artist's IP and authentication rights post-death (Warhol Foundation, Calder Foundation, etc.).

Tech-side attempts: Tagsmart (RFID + database), Verisart (blockchain authentication), Artory (blockchain registry), Portion (NFT provenance). The blockchain layer was hyped 2017–2022, mostly hasn't taken root in the traditional market because the trust model is wrong — auction houses and foundations don't want to delegate authentication to a public chain.

This layer is structurally underbuilt for digital-native and contemporary work: no good answer for "is this digital edition the legitimate one." Authentication will likely fragment: per-artist registries (Plataforma's natural extension), per-platform registries (Artsy maintaining its own), and a long tail of unauthenticated works that exist in legal gray zones.

Layer 6 — Pricing + market data

What things are worth.

Artprice (subscription, ~$300–1k/yr, claims to track 30M+ auction records since 1700) and Artnet Price Database (subscription, ~14M auction records). These are the deepest commercial datasets on art prices.

Free-ish: Sotheby's and Christie's websites publish recent results but not bulk-extractable. Mutualart's artist pages aggregate auction data but paywall the deep history.

Critical insight: prices in the primary market (galleries) are not public. Only secondary-market (auction) prices are publicly observable. This means the entire Layer 4 aggregation business has a partial-information problem — they can show you what the work is, but for primary-market work they can't show you what it costs without the gallery's say-so. Artsy's "request price" buttons are the workaround; the primary market resists transparent pricing because galleries make money from negotiating opacity.

Where everyone takes a cut

Artist  → gets ~50% of primary sale + 0% of secondary sales
          (US doesn't have artist resale royalty; EU has limited resale right)

Gallery → takes 50% of primary sale + ~$3-7k/yr to list on Artsy
          + ~$5-15k/yr Artlogic / ArtBinder subscription
          + 10-25% to Artsy on online primary sales

Auction → 26-28% buyer's premium + 5-15% seller's commission
          + retains lifetime data rights on every sale

Aggreg. → ~10-25% transaction commission + listing fees
          + opaque pricing-data licensing back to subscribers

Pricing → $300-1k/year subscription per user

Buyer   → pays everyone above

The artist sees value drain at every secondary transaction (someone resells their work, takes profit, the artist gets nothing in most jurisdictions). This is the political opening Plataforma names: "if value is created, it flows to you" is structurally hard in the existing market because the existing market is built around extracting from each transaction without crediting the upstream creator.

Where the structural openings are

Open #1 — The cooperative discovery layer. Artsy and Artnet are private companies that extract from artists and galleries. A cooperatively-governed alternative — Plataforma — has a real opening if it can solve cold-start (the Pod-based onboarding from the founders' concept) and offer something the commercial discovery layer can't (artist-owned data, exportable identity, no algorithmic feed gaming). The auto-populate from museum collections solves Plataforma's cold-start: even before any artist signs up, the directory is populated with public-domain work and known-living-artist references.

Open #2 — ORCID-for-artists. The identity layer is fragmented across ULAN, ISNI, Wikidata QID, MusicBrainz, IMDb. None is artist-controlled or universally adopted. Whoever lands the standard identity has substantial leverage — every data source upstream and every sales platform downstream needs to reconcile against a single ID.

Open #3 — Embedding-based discovery. The entire market currently runs on tags + categories + curator labels. Visual-similarity-at-scale (Beowolff) is genuinely new — Pinterest is the only widespread example, and Pinterest doesn't structure the discovery around artist-as-entity.

Open #4 — Resale royalty infrastructure. EU's droit de suite gives artists 0.5–4% of resale price for ~70 years post-creation; US has nothing. A platform that operationalizes resale tracking (tracking every secondary sale, computing royalties, distributing to artists or estates) creates a new revenue stream where none currently exists.

Open #5 — Authentication for digital-native and contemporary work. Traditional market won't delegate to blockchain; per-artist registries (running on cooperative infrastructure like Plataforma) might be the right scale. Each artist signs off on their own work; the platform just hosts the registry.
