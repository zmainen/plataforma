The signal problem

The road-meets-rubber question for any visual art recommendation system: can it actually surface artists that people will love?

This is a harder problem than it looks, because the existing market gives you almost nothing to learn from.

The data sparsity is roughly 100,000× worse than Spotify's

A back-of-envelope:

SpotifyArt market
Yearly events~1.5 trillion plays~5–15M transactions
Average user events/year~10,000 plays~0 (most users never buy)
Same user, repeat eventsconstant (skip, replay, save, share, playlist)rare
Implicit signaldensealmost none

Sales as a recommendation signal is structurally unworkable. Even Artsy's data — listing views, "request price" clicks, gallery follows — is sparse compared to Spotify's stream-level event log. It's enough to model collectors who already buy a lot; it's not enough to recommend to a new user, and the long tail of artists has zero signal at all.

So the question is: where else does the signal come from?

Where the signal could come from

Six options, roughly ranked by quality:

  1. Dwell time + scroll behavior on a viewing destination. This is Pinterest's secret. People go to the platform to look; the platform tracks what they look at, what they linger on, what they save. Dense signal, no purchase required. Requires building a destination people return to.
  1. Saves / pins / bookmarks. Lighter than purchase, denser than purchase. Pinterest scaled this to billions. Plataforma's "follow this artist" flow is the equivalent.
  1. Search queries + click-through. People search artists they know; the set of names typed reveals taste. Beowolff's "find similar to this image" flow is unusually rich because the image is the query — much higher information than a name.
  1. Cold-start declarations. Ask new users for 3–5 artists they love. Spotify does this; Pandora's thumb-up/thumb-down was this. Crude but bootstraps the model.
  1. Ambient public signal. Wikipedia article views per artist (publicly available), Google Trends per artist name, museum visit data. Coarse but covers every artist with any historical presence.
  1. Auction prices as proxy. Price isn't preference, but high price correlates with both "many people wanted this work" and "the buyer is somebody whose taste matters." Sotheby's + Artnet's price databases are the closest existing dataset.

The real recommendation pipeline blends 4–5 of these. Cold-start with declarations + bootstrap with dwell time on a destination + augment with search + correct with public-signal smoothing. That's the architecture. None of it works without #1 — the destination.

How Pinterest makes money

~$3.6B annual revenue (2024), ~480M monthly active users. Breakdown:

  • ~95% advertising — "promoted pins" mixed into the algorithmic feed, sold via auction-based bidding to advertisers (mostly retail, home goods, fashion, beauty)
  • ~5% shopping affiliate / direct integrations — when a pin links to a buyable product, Pinterest takes a small cut
  • 0% subscription — there is no Pinterest Premium

Operating margin: 5–10% in good years, sometimes negative. Not actually a great business. Pinterest has been a "near-IPO darling, now treading water" story for a decade. It built one of the most sophisticated visual-recommendation systems in the world and monetizes it through ads at a margin lower than most retailers.

The reason Pinterest's economics aren't great is the same reason Spotify's aren't great for artists: attention monetization extracts a flat rate on engagement and doesn't capture the value of high-intent traffic well. Someone planning a wedding generates 100× the commercial value of someone bored-scrolling, but Pinterest can only capture the difference at the margin. Most of the value goes to the brands that buy the ads.

For Plataforma, Pinterest's monetization model is the wrong one. Same trap as Spotify: optimize for attention, capture small fraction, leak most of the value to advertisers / labels. Better target economics:

ModelExampleFit for Plataforma
Institutional subscriptionJSTOR, ARTstor, Bloomberg TerminalHigh — museums + research institutions + serious collectors will pay $100s–$1000s/yr for accurate data + advanced search
Affiliate commerceBandcamp's gallery cutMedium — when sales happen the platform takes a small cut without owning inventory
Freemium with exportGitHub, ORCID + paid premiumMedium — base directory is free; advanced features (CSV export, alerts, API) paid
Per-artist "verified profile" feeBandcamp Pro, Patreon ProLow — risks recreating Artsy's listing-fee dynamic
Ads on browsing surfacePinterest, InstagramBad — recreates the attention extraction we're trying to escape

JSTOR-for-art is probably the right shape: subscription-priced data infrastructure for institutional users who need accurate, deep, queryable art data. That's an underexploited model — only Artprice / Artnet PDB occupy it, and badly.

Pinterest's relevance — pattern, not business

What Pinterest proved:

  • Visual-similarity recommendation at scale works. Their "Visual Search" was one of the first major image-embedding systems deployed publicly, ~2017.
  • People will browse images for hours if the recommendation surface is good.
  • Pinning/saving as engagement signal is dense enough to power recommendation.
  • The infinite-scroll grid of imagery is the right UX shape for visual discovery.

What Pinterest got wrong for art:

  • No artist-as-entity structure. "Modern art" boards lump Picasso, Etsy prints, and dorm posters.
  • No provenance, no authentication.
  • No commerce path appropriate for the art market.
  • No respect for artist rights — images get re-pinned without attribution; artists hate it.

A useful framing: Plataforma is "Pinterest for art that respects artists, with provenance, structured around artist identity rather than mood boards." Take the engine; reject the rights structure.

Are there places people go online to look at art?

Honest answer: there is no major online destination structured around looking at art for its own sake. It's a real gap, and it's why Plataforma's "directory worth exploring from day one" framing is grounded.

The biggest of these by far is Instagram, and Instagram's data is locked. The biggest open art-specific destination is WikiArt, and WikiArt's UX is essentially a 2010 Wikipedia clone — no recommendation, no taste graph, no tracking, no decent search. It's a reference, not a place to spend time.

That gap is the opportunity. There is room — actual product-shaped room — for a "place online where you go to look at art, where what you look at builds your taste graph, where the system learns you and shows you the next thing you'd love." Plataforma's directory could be that destination. Beowolff-grade visual similarity could be its recommendation surface. Bandcamp/JSTOR-style economics could fund it without recreating the extraction.

The actual product question

The road-meets-rubber moment isn't "can visual search find similar work" — that's solved technology. It's: can we build a destination people return to often enough to generate the signal that makes the recommendations excellent?

The honest test: would you, personally, open a "browse art" tab on your phone for ten minutes a day instead of Instagram? Most people don't currently do that, not because they don't want to, but because nowhere good exists. Building the place — beautiful, structured around artists, free of ads and influencer noise, with a recommendation surface that actually surfaces what you'd love — is the product problem.

The data infrastructure (museum survey, adapters, MoMA resolver) is the substrate; the destination is the actual unmet need.

That's also the strongest case in any conversation with commercial discovery players (Artsy, Artnet, the merged entity): Artsy is a marketplace people visit when they're already looking to buy. Plataforma builds the place people visit when they want to look. Different intent surface, different signal, different growth shape. The two are complementary, not competitive — Artsy converts intent to purchase; Plataforma builds intent over time.
