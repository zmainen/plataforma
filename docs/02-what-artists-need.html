What artists actually need

Three things. None of them are a better app.

Control over your own record. Right now, an artist's professional presence is scattered across five or six surfaces — a portfolio site, an Instagram account, an Are.na channel, gallery profiles they can't edit, a CV saved as a PDF that goes stale the moment it's finished, a Wikipedia stub written by someone they've never met. None of these talk to each other. None of them is the canonical, complete, up-to-date truth about who the artist is and what they've made.

When someone wants to find you, they Google your name and get a junk drawer: a defunct gallery page, a few auction results from years ago, an Instagram post that doesn't represent your current work. There is no authoritative you online — and the closest thing, your gallery's website, shows only what they choose, drops your older work when they take on someone new, and controls the version of you the world sees.

What artists need is simpler than what they have: one place where their work, history, exhibitions, and institutional traces live in a form they maintain — findable, exportable, and not owned by anyone else.

Professional connection. Artists build extraordinary relationships in person — with collaborators, curators, other artists, institutions. Those connections are the lifeblood of a creative career. And they are almost entirely invisible online. There is no shared professional space where you can see who's done what, who has worked with whom, what's happening next. The connections are real and deep. The infrastructure to make them visible and useful doesn't exist.

Other creative fields have this. Musicians have organizations that aggregate their interests, track their credits, and make their professional networks legible. Scientists have institutional directories and shared publication records. Artists have a gallery (maybe), a foundation (after death), and whatever they manage to maintain themselves. The richness of artistic collaboration — one of the most interconnected creative communities in the world — has no digital reflection.

Collective leverage. This is the deepest need and the least discussed. Artists individually negotiate against aggregated counterparties — galleries, auction houses, platforms, institutions — and lose, repeatedly, asymmetrically. The gallery negotiates with hundreds of artists; the artist negotiates with one gallery. The platform sets terms for thousands of users; each user accepts or leaves. The power imbalance is structural, not personal. It's not that galleries are bad actors. It's that atomized individuals cannot negotiate effectively against organized counterparties. This is basic economics — and every other creative field figured it out decades ago.

What's missing isn't a tool. It's the infrastructure for collective action — a way for artists to pool their connections, their data, their professional knowledge, and their negotiating position. Not a union exactly, but something with the same structural function: a cooperative that aggregates artists' interests so they stop negotiating alone.

These three needs — control, connection, leverage — are what Plataforma is built to serve. Everything else follows from them.
