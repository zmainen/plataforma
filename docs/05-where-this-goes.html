Where this goes

Plataforma starts in New York City — with artists, institutions, and audiences drawn from the founders' own networks. Small enough to learn from, connected enough to demonstrate the model. The first Pods will come from existing relationships, existing collaborations, existing trust. That's deliberate. You can't build cooperative infrastructure on a foundation of strangers.

But what the pilot is building toward is larger than a directory.

Professional infrastructure artists never had. If the cooperative model works — if artists join, if the directory becomes the place people go to find out about an artist — it becomes something the art world has never had: shared professional infrastructure owned by the people it serves. The equivalent of what musicians built with their performing rights organizations, what scientists built with ORCID and institutional repositories, what open-source developers built with platforms they govern. Not a product to be used. Infrastructure to be relied on.

The difference matters. Products get acquired, pivoted, shut down. Infrastructure persists because the community that depends on it maintains it. That's the long-term bet: Plataforma becomes load-bearing — the thing institutions, researchers, curators, and collectors reconcile against because it's the most reliable record of who made what.

A place to look. There is no major place online to just look at art — not to buy, not to scroll an algorithm, just to spend time with work you'd love and discover artists you didn't know. Instagram is closed and algorithmic. Image boards mix fine art with everything else. The closest dedicated destination is a bare-bones reference site from 2010. The gap is striking. For music, books, film — there are beautiful, navigable destinations designed around the experience of browsing and discovering. For art, there's almost nothing.

A directory worth exploring — structured around artists, beautifully designed, free of advertising and influencer noise, with a recommendation surface that actually learns what you love — is not a crowded market. It's an empty one. Building the place people return to, not to buy but to look, is both the cultural ambition and the practical foundation: the more people look, the better the system understands what to show them, and the more valuable the directory becomes for everyone — artists, institutions, and audiences alike.

What collective power makes possible. The directory is the foundation. What artists build on it is theirs to decide. With connections made visible and collective leverage made real, things become possible that weren't before: mentoring networks that aren't mediated by institutions. Shared professional knowledge about contracts, residencies, grants, legal resources. Organizing around specific issues — fair licensing, data rights, institutional accountability — with the weight of a member-governed cooperative behind it.

Eventually, the leverage to negotiate fair terms with the platforms and institutions that currently set terms unilaterally. Not because the cooperative has market power in the traditional sense, but because it holds the most complete, most trusted, most current record of creative work — and that record has value to everyone in the ecosystem.

That's the long game. A cooperative directory that starts as a tool for artists to control their own professional record, grows into the infrastructure the art world reconciles against, and becomes the foundation for collective action that artists have never had the structure to sustain.

It starts with a Pod of artists who already trust each other, in one city, building the first version of something they own. Where it goes from there depends on them.
