Why now

Artists have always worked inside structures that extract from them. The gallery takes half of every primary sale. The auction house profits when your work is resold — you get nothing. After you die, a foundation controls your intellectual property and decides what counts as authentic. These arrangements aren't new. What's new is that artists can finally see the whole picture.

The latest chapter is AI. Your images are scraped without consent and fed into training data. Algorithmic feeds reward gaming over practice. Platforms offer visibility in exchange for reach they monetize and you can't bank. The fear this generates is real, but the dynamic isn't novel — it's the same one that produced the 50/50 gallery split, the resale market that cuts artists out, and the social-media treadmill that trades your time for someone else's revenue. AI just made the pattern undeniable.

At the same time, the pandemic made something else visible: how little infrastructure exists to support artists when things go wrong. No centralized professional resource. No shared knowledge base. No collective leverage when grants are cut, institutions restructure, or platforms change their terms. Artists in New York and everywhere else found themselves individually navigating a system that treats them as raw material — talented, essential, and structurally alone.

But something shifted. Artists who would not have organized around fair contracts five years ago are organizing now — around data rights, around ownership, around the basic question of who controls the record of their work. The threat of AI didn't create new problems. It made old problems politically urgent for the first time in a generation.

That urgency is the opening. Not because the technology to build something better is new — it isn't. Because the willingness is. Artists are ready to build collective infrastructure for themselves, and they're no longer willing to wait for the market to do it for them.

Plataforma exists because this is the moment a cooperative becomes possible to build. The window won't stay open forever. The question is whether artists use it to create infrastructure they own, or whether the next platform captures the organizing energy and extracts from it the way every previous one has.

We think artists should own it.
