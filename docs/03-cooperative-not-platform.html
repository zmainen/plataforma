A cooperative, not a platform

The distinction matters more than it sounds.

A platform is a business that provides a service. It has investors, a growth imperative, and the structural freedom to change its terms whenever the math requires it. Every art platform currently operating — listings sites, marketplace aggregators, social networks — extracts from artists through listing fees, transaction commissions, or attention monetization. Each one launched with promises about serving artists. The promises last until they conflict with the business model.

A cooperative is governed by its members. The people whose work constitutes the directory make the rules. It can't be acquired and have its commitments reversed. It can't pivot to an ad model because a board of directors decided growth was stalling. The governance structure is the credibility of every promise the organization makes. Without it, "artists own their data" is marketing copy.

Plataforma is built on five structural commitments — not terms-of-service promises, but design decisions enforced by cooperative governance:

You own your catalog. The platform is a custodian, not an owner. You grant a revocable license to display and index your work. If you leave, your data is deleted — not archived, not retained for future use, not kept "in case you come back."

You can export everything, anytime. Your complete catalog — metadata, images, relationships, exhibition history — is exportable in open formats. No lock-in. If something better comes along, or if Plataforma fails to serve you, you leave with everything you brought.

No training without your consent. If the platform ever builds recommendation tools or any form of intelligence from artist data, you opt in explicitly. Consent is granular and revocable. This is the commitment that matters most in the current moment — and the one no existing platform makes credibly, because no existing platform has the governance structure to enforce it.

If value is created, it flows to you. If the platform ever generates revenue from your data — through institutional subscriptions, licensing, or any other mechanism — compensation is tracked and distributed to the members whose work created the value. The cooperative doesn't capture surplus from its members.

Artists govern the platform. Policies are set by members. Not by investors, not by a product team optimizing for engagement, not by an algorithm. By the people whose creative work the whole thing rests on.

These commitments are only as strong as the structure that enforces them. That's why the cooperative form isn't a nice-to-have — it's the product. It's the thing that makes every other promise believable.

The cooperative grows through trust, not viral acquisition. Small cohorts of artists who already know and work with each other — Pods — join as a group, anchored by a leader who's an early adopter. The network builds from real relationships outward, not from a sign-up page inward. This is slower than a venture-backed launch. It's also the only way to build something artists will actually trust with their professional lives.
