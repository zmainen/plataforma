How it looks from the inside

To the artist

You make the work. Then you maintain a Squarespace site, an Instagram, an Are.na channel, a Cargo Collective archive, scattered gallery profiles you can't edit, a CV PDF that's out of date the moment you save it, and a Wikipedia stub written by someone you don't know. None of these talk to each other. Your representation lives in five places at three levels of currency, and updating any of them is unpaid administrative labor that comes out of your studio time.

When someone wants to know what you've made, they Google your name and get a junk drawer: an old gallery's defunct page, the Wikipedia stub, an Instagram post from 2019, an artnet listing of two auction results from 2014, the wrong Tumblr. There is no canonical "you" online. The closest thing — the gallery that represents you — controls the version that's seen, takes 50% of any primary sale, and quietly stops showing your old work the moment they take on a more profitable artist.

You can't see who owns the work after it leaves the studio. You can't see what auction prices your secondary work brings (you stop being a party to the transaction once the gallery sells once). You don't get resale royalties in most jurisdictions. You don't know which museums hold your work until a curator emails you years later. You don't know who's writing about you. You don't know which collectors are looking for new work in your medium right now. All the information that would help you make a living from your practice is held by other people, and the structural deal is that you don't get to see it.

The AI threat is the latest version of an old story. Your images are scraped without consent for training data; algorithmic feeds reward different rhythms than your practice can sustain; "build your brand" platforms extract from your reach in exchange for visibility you can't bank. The terror of it isn't novel — it's the same dynamic that gave you the gallery 50% split, the foundation that controls your IP after death, and the auction house that profits from your fame without crediting you for the run-up. AI is the current chapter of a long book.

What you'd actually want: one place where your work, history, contacts, and traces in institutional collections live in a form you maintain, that's findable, exportable, and that doesn't take a cut every time someone discovers you. Plus, ideally, the ability to see — even partially — what the market is doing with your work after it leaves you.

To the potential buyer

You see something at a fair, on Instagram, on a friend's wall. You want to know more about the artist. You search. You get half a Wikipedia article, a few mismatched Artsy listings (most "request price"), a gallery website with three works visible (none with prices), and scattered auction results across three databases that each charge $300/year for the deep version. Comparing what you've seen across four artists takes hours of clicking.

You DM the gallery. If they recognize your name, you get a friendly response and a PDF in three days. If you're new, you get nothing or a polite brush-off. The price you're eventually quoted depends on whether the gallery thinks you're a serious collector or a tourist; you have no way to verify that the price is real. The provenance is a folder of papers handed over with the bill.

You can't browse the way you browse music or books. There's no "this artist resembles that artist" navigation that doesn't run through someone's editorial taste. There's no "people who bought this also bought that" that doesn't make you feel like you're shopping at a flea market. The art world's relationship-based gatekeeping makes you feel unwelcome unless you've been there a decade or you arrived with a wealth advisor.

What you'd actually want: a trustworthy directory of who exists, what they've made, where their work is. A discovery layer that connects across galleries + auction houses + museums + artist sites, indexing them coherently. Some level of pricing context — even just "comparable recent sales" — so you don't feel like you're flying blind. A way to find artists whose work you'd love, based on the works you already love, without needing to know a dealer who'll guide you.

What both sides converge on

The artist and the buyer are both losing the same thing: a coherent, discoverable, persistent sense of the artist's body of work, accessible to both of them on equal terms. The current infrastructure splits this artifact between gatekeepers — the gallery's view (curated for sales), the auction house's view (curated for resales), the foundation's view (curated for legacy), the museum's view (curated for collections), the artist's own view (intermittently maintained). None of them is the canonical, navigable, public truth.

Plataforma's claim is that the canonical view should exist, that it should be artist-owned, that it should be free to browse for the buyer, and that the discovery layer it enables should serve both sides without taking a cut on the moments where they meet.
